A History of Australian Poetry

Australian poetry has a long and rich history dating back to the 19th century. During this time, poets began to explore themes and subjects unique to the Australian landscape, such as the ruggedness of the bush, the harshness of the outback, and the relationship between people and the land.

One of the most famous Australian poets of the 19th century was Banjo Patterson, whose works such as The Man From Snowy River and Clancy of the Overflow have become iconic representations of Australian culture and identity.

In the 20th century, Australian poetry continued to evolve, with poets like Dorothea MacKellar and Oodgeroo Noonuccal exploring issues related to national identity, multiculturalism, and Indigenous rights. Today, Australian poetry is a vibrant and diverse art form that continues to reflect the complexities and contradictions of the Australian experience.

The Man From Snowy River

The Man From Snowy River is a poem by Banjo Patterson, first published in The Bulletin magazine in 1890. The poem tells the story of a horseback pursuit to recapture a valuable colt that has escaped into the wilds of the Snowy Mountains. The poem is considered a quintessential representation of the Australian bush and its people.

Banjo Patterson was born in 1864 and is considered one of Australia's most famous poets. He wrote many other famous poems, like Waltzing Matilda and Clancy of the Overflow.

My Country

My Country is a poem by Dorothea MacKellar, first published in 1908. The poem celebrates the beauty and diversity of the Australian landscape, and expresses the author's love for her country. The poem is often quoted in nationalistic contexts and has become a symbol of Australian identity.

Dorothea MacKellar was born in 1885 and grew up on a property near Gunnedah in New South Wales. She wrote many other famous poems, including Core of My Heart and Colours of the Light.

We Are Going

We Are Going is a poem by Oodgeroo Noonuccal, first published in 1964. The poem explores the displacement and marginalization of Indigenous Australians in the wake of European colonization. It is considered one of the most important works of Indigenous Australian poetry and a powerful statement of resistance and identity.

Oodgeroo Noonuccal, formerly known as Kath Walker, was born in 1920 on Stradbroke Island in Queensland. She was a prominent activist for Indigenous rights and a prolific writer, publishing many works of poetry, fiction, and non-fiction.

Timeline of Australian Poetry

1800s

Early colonial poetry in Australia is predominantly written by British settlers, focusing on themes of nature, patriotism, and colonialism. Notable works from this time include "The Convict's Tour to Hell" by Charles Tompson and "The Wild Colonial Boy" by anonymous.

1860

Adam Lindsay Gordon publishes his first collection of poems, "Sea Spray and Smoke Drift," which features a mix of bush ballads and more introspective works. Gordon becomes known as one of the leading poets of the Australian bush.

1885

Early colonial poetry in Australia is predominantly written by British settlers, focusing on themes of nature, patriotism, and colonialism. Notable works from this time include "The Convict's Tour to Hell" by Charles Tompson and "The Wild Colonial Boy" by anonymous.

1890s

The "Bulletin School" of Australian poetry emerges, featuring writers such as Henry Lawson and A.B. "Banjo" Paterson. This movement focuses on the experiences of working-class Australians and is characterized by its use of colloquial language and bush settings.

1901

The Commonwealth of Australia is established, and with it comes a renewed interest in Australian identity and culture. This leads to a surge in patriotic poetry and works that celebrate the natural beauty of the country.

1915

Rupert Brooke's poem "The Soldier" inspires a wave of war poetry in Australia, as the country's troops are sent to fight in World War I. Notable works from this time include "In Flanders Fields" by John McCrae and "For the Fallen" by Laurence Binyon.

1920s

The "Jindyworobak Movement" emerges, advocating for a return to Indigenous Australian culture and language in literature. Writers associated with this movement include Rex Ingamells and Mary Gilmore.

1930s

The Great Depression prompts a new wave of social and political commentary in Australian poetry. Notable works from this time include Kenneth Slessor's "Five Bells" and Max Harris's "The Angry Penguins."

1950s

The "New Wave" of Australian poetry emerges, featuring writers such as Judith Wright, A.D. Hope, and James McAuley. This movement is characterized by its focus on modernist techniques and themes of alienation, existentialism, and urbanization.

1960s

A new generation of poets emerges, including Les Murray, Gwen Harwood, and Bruce Dawe. These writers focus on the experiences of everyday Australians and address social issues such as feminism, Vietnam War protests, and Indigenous rights.

1980s

Performance poetry becomes popular in Australia, with events such as the Nimbin Poetry Festival and the Sydney Poetry Olympics. Poets associated with this movement include Candy Royalle, PiO, and Michael Farrell.

1990s

The "Martian" poetry movement emerges, featuring writers such as John Kinsella and Peter Porter. This movement is characterized by its use of science fiction and surrealism to explore contemporary issues.

2000s

The rise of digital media and social networking platforms leads to a new generation of poets who use these technologies to share and promote their work. Notable online poets from Australia include Omar Sakr and Hera Lindsay Bird.

2010s

The #MeToo movement inspires a new wave of feminist poetry in Australia, with writers such as Ellen van Neerven, Fiona Wright, and Alison Whittaker addressing issues of gender, sexuality, and power in their work.

The Man From Snowy River

There was movement at the station, for the word had passed around
That the colt from Old Regret had got away,
And had joined the wild bush horses - he was worth a thousand pound,
So all the cracks had gathered to the fray.
All the tried and noted riders from the stations near and far
Had mustered at the homestead overnight,
For the bushmen love hard riding where the wild bush horses are,
And the stock-horse snuffs the battle with delight.

There was Harrison, who made his pile when Pardon won the cup,
The old man with his hair as white as snow;
But few could ride beside him when his blood was fairly up —
He would go wherever horse and man could go.
And Clancy of the Overflow came down to lend a hand,
No better horseman ever held the reins;
For never horse could throw him while the saddle girths would stand,
He learnt to ride while droving on the plains.

And one was there, a stripling on a small and weedy beast;
He was something like a racehorse undersized,
With a touch of Timor pony — three parts thoroughbred at least —
And such as are by mountain horsemen prized.
He was hard and tough and wiry — just the sort that won't say die —
There was courage in his quick impatient tread;
And he bore the badge of gameness in his bright and fiery eye,
And the proud and lofty carriage of his head.

My Country

The love of field and coppice,
Of green and shaded lanes.
Of ordered woods and gardens
Is running in your veins,
Strong love of grey-blue distance
Brown streams and soft dim skies
I know but cannot share it,
My love is otherwise.

I love a sunburnt country,
A land of sweeping plains,
Of ragged mountain ranges,
Of droughts and flooding rains.
I love her far horizons,
I love her jewel-sea,
Her beauty and her terror -
The wide brown land for me!

A stark white ring-barked forest
All tragic to the moon,
The sapphire-misted mountains,
The hot gold hush of noon.
Green tangle of the brushes,
Where lithe lianas coil,
And orchids deck the tree-tops
And ferns the warm dark soil.

We Are Going

They came in to the little town
A semi-naked band subdued and silent
All that remained of their tribe.
They came here to the place of their old bora ground
Where now the many white men hurry about like ants.
Notice of the estate agent reads: 'Rubbish May Be Tipped Here'.
Now it half covers the traces of the old bora ring.
'We are as strangers here now, but the white tribe are the strangers.
We belong here, we are of the old ways.
We are the corroboree and the bora ground,
We are the old ceremonies, the laws of the elders.
We are the wonder tales of Dream Time, the tribal legends told.
We are the past, the hunts and the laughing games, the wandering camp fires.

We are the lightening bolt over Gaphembah Hill
Quick and terrible,
And the Thunderer after him, that loud fellow.
We are the quiet daybreak paling the dark lagoon.
We are the shadow-ghosts creeping back as the camp fires burn low. We are nature and the past, all the old ways
Gone now and scattered.
The scrubs are gone, the hunting and the laughter.
The eagle is gone, the emu and the kangaroo are gone from this place. The bora ring is gone.
The corroboree is gone.
And we are going.'
